1 - Self-Attention

From Attention Is All You Need (Transformer). Click a word to see what it attends to.

What's happening: Self-attention lets every token in a sequence "look at" all other tokens at once, and computes a set of attention weights for each (softmax-normalized, summing to 1). A higher weight means that token matters more to the current word. This replaces RNN recurrence, making parallel training and long-range dependency capture possible. Paper: The Transformer

Weights are softmax-normalized, so they sum to 1. Try "it" — notice it leans toward "cat" and "mat".

2 - Mixture of Experts

From Mixtral of Experts. A router sends each token to the top-2 of 4 experts.

What's happening: MoE splits one big model into many "expert" sub-networks, but each token only activates a few of them (top-2 here). A lightweight router decides the routing. That means the total parameter count can be huge, while compute per forward pass grows only with the activated experts — the key to "more parameters without being slower." Paper: Mixtral

Only a fraction of experts activate per token — that's why MoE stays cheap at inference.

3 - Scaling Laws

From Scaling Laws & Chinchilla. Drag to grow the model and its data.

What's happening: Large models follow a remarkably clean rule: as you scale up parameters (N), data (D), and compute (C) together, validation loss drops smoothly along a power law. Chinchilla went further — most models were "under-fed on data," and D and N should grow roughly 1:20 together rather than piling on parameters alone. Papers: Scaling Laws · Chinchilla

4 - Retrieval-Augmented Generation

From RAG. Pick a question; the system retrieves the closest notes, then answers from them.

What's happening: RAG separates a model’s "memory" from an external knowledge base: it first retrieves the most relevant document snippets for the user’s question, then stitches them into the prompt so the model answers from that evidence. This cuts hallucinations and lets you update knowledge without retraining. Paper: RAG

Similarity here is plain word overlap (Jaccard) — enough to show the retrieve-then-read loop.

5 - Chain-of-Thought

From Chain-of-Thought Prompting. Letting the model reason step by step unlocks hard problems.

What's happening: Asked for a direct answer, a model often trips up on the intermediate steps; but add a line like "let’s think step by step" and it will spell out the reasoning chain before giving the conclusion. The essence of CoT is having the model first generate "reasoning tokens" that break a hard problem into verifiable small steps — which sharply lifts accuracy on arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning. Paper: Chain-of-Thought

6 - Tree-of-Thoughts

From Tree of Thoughts. Explore many reasoning branches, score them, keep the best.

What's happening: CoT has only a single reasoning line — if it goes wrong, everything fails. ToT turns reasoning into a tree: multiple candidate "thoughts" branch out from the problem, each step is scored / self-evaluated, weak branches are pruned, and the strongest is expanded further, finally backtracking to the best solution. This gives the model "planning + backtracking" power, ideal for hard search problems. Paper: Tree of Thoughts

7 - RLHF vs DPO

From InstructGPT / RLHF & DPO. A reward model ranks responses; the policy learns to match preferences.

What's happening: Making a model "helpful, useful, and safe" is about preference alignment. The recipe: take human rankings of which of two answers is better to train a "reward model," then push the policy toward higher rewards. RLHF needs two stages — reward model + PPO reinforcement learning; DPO is cleverer — it derives a classification-style loss directly from the preference pairs in one step, skipping both the reward model and the RL loop. Papers: RLHF (original) · InstructGPT · DPO

8 - LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)

From LoRA. Fine-tune a tiny low-rank update instead of the whole weight matrix.

What's happening: Full fine-tuning updates the entire weight matrix W (d×d) — an enormous parameter count. LoRA’s insight is that the "change" ΔW during fine-tuning is usually low-rank: it can be approximated by the product of two thin matrices B (d×r) and A (r×d), and you only train those two (params = 2·d·r, with r tiny). The base model stays frozen; to switch tasks you just swap the small LoRA tail — fast and cheap. Paper: LoRA

9 - RoPE (Rotary Position Embedding)

From RoFormer. Rotate token vectors by angle θ·position so attention depends on relative position.

What's happening: A Transformer has no built-in sense of word order. RoPE’s elegant trick: rotate each position’s query/key vector by an angle θ·position. Because the inner product of rotated vectors depends only on the "position difference" (m−n) rather than absolute position, attention naturally carries relative-position information — which is why LLaMA / Mistral / Qwen use it by default. Paper: RoPE

10 - BPE Tokenization

From GPT-2 / GPT-3 tokenizers. Byte-Pair Encoding merges the most frequent character pairs, again and again.

What's happening: A model can’t read "words," only tokens. BPE starts from single characters and repeatedly merges the "most frequent adjacent character pair" into a new token (e.g. t+o→to, to+k→tok…) until it hits the vocabulary size. Common words become short tokens, rare words split into fragments — which is why "non-English / gibberish" gets chopped into many small pieces and costs more tokens. Paper: GPT-3 (tokenizer)

11 - Sampling: temperature, top-p & top-k

A language model outputs logits. Sampling is the recipe that turns them into a chosen next word.

① The candidate logits

Imagine the prompt ended and the model must pick the next word. It scores every word in its vocabulary; here are eight candidates with their raw logits. Higher logit = the model "prefers" that word. These are fixed for this sandbox — play with the controls below to see how the same logits become very different behaviors.

② Carve the distribution

What's happening: logits are turned into probabilities by softmax, then three knobs reshape them. Temperature divides the logits first — low T sharpens (confident), high T flattens (random). Top-k keeps only the k most likely words. Top-p (nucleus) keeps the smallest set of words whose probabilities sum to at least p. Anything left is zeroed and the rest renormalized.

③ Draw a word

Sampling means drawing one token from the final distribution. Hit the button a few times at high temperature (try T = 2, top-p = 1) and you'll get variety; at T = 0.2 or top-k = 1 the model becomes a broken record.

All numbers are real softmax / nucleus computations over the eight fixed logits above — this is exactly what a decoder does at inference, just with a vocabulary of millions instead of eight.

12 - Softmax & temperature, viscerally

The function that turns a vector of logits into a probability distribution - and the temperature dial that controls how confident it is.

① The logits

What's happening: before softmax, the model holds raw, unbounded scores called logits. Edit any of them and watch the distribution below react. Raising one logit lifts its probability and steals mass from the others.
softmax(z)i = ezi / Σj ezj

② Temperature

What's happening: in practice we divide the logits by a temperature T before softmax. As T → 0 the distribution collapses onto the largest logit (a confident, almost one-hot choice). As T → ∞ every word becomes equally likely. Sweep the slider and feel the model go from decisive to indecisive.
1.0

These are the exact same probabilities the Sampling sandbox starts from — sampling just adds top-p / top-k and the random draw on top.

13 - Word embeddings & analogies

Embeddings put words into a space where meaning is distance. The famous trick: do arithmetic on the vectors.

① Pick an analogy

What's happening: every word below is a point in a 2-D plane (a deliberately tiny "embedding"). The surprising fact about real embeddings is that king − man + woman lands almost exactly on queen. Here the space is hand-built so the arithmetic is exact — a toy, but the principle is the one that powers word2vec and friends.

14 - The attention matrix, laid bare

Self-attention is just a grid of weights. Every row says "for this word, how much do I look at every other word?"

① Type a sentence

What's happening: each token gets a 4-D vector (here from a fixed, illustrative embedding that also encodes word position). We score every query·key pair, divide by √d, and softmax each row. The result is a proper distribution — every row sums to 1. Hover any cell for its exact weight.
Hover a cell to read its weight.

Embeddings are deterministic but random-by-design (not trained), so this shows the shape of an attention matrix, not a real model's beliefs. Try "the dog and the cat played" to see position pull weight toward nearby words.
