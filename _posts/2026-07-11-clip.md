**Paper:** Radford, A., et al. (OpenAI), *Learning Transferable Visual Models From Natural Language Supervision*, ICML 2021. [arXiv:2103.00020](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.00020)

CLIP changed what "understanding an image" costs. Instead of hiring people to label millions of pictures with fixed classes, OpenAI pulled **400 million (image, text) pairs** off the internet and trained a model to match an image to its caption. The payoff is a visual representation that transfers to *any* downstream task through natural language, with **zero task-specific training**. It is also the image encoder behind DALL·E, Stable Diffusion, and nearly every vision-language model worth mentioning. For the LLM era, it is the bridge from text to pixels.

## The trick: contrastive pretraining

Two encoders, an image tower $I(\cdot)$ and a text tower $T(\cdot)$, embed into a shared space. For a batch of $N$ pairs, pull the matching (image, text) closer and push the $N^2 - N$ non-matching pairs apart with the InfoNCE contrastive loss:

$$
\mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\left[
\log \frac{e^{\,\mathrm{sim}(I_i, T_i)/\tau}}{\sum_{j=1}^{N} e^{\,\mathrm{sim}(I_i, T_j)/\tau}}
+
\log \frac{e^{\,\mathrm{sim}(T_i, I_i)/\tau}}{\sum_{j=1}^{N} e^{\,\mathrm{sim}(T_i, I_j)/\tau}}
\right]
$$

where $\mathrm{sim}$ is cosine similarity and $\tau$ a temperature. There is no fixed label set. Supervision is just the caption.

## Zero-shot transfer

Because text is the classifier, you never retrain. For a new task you write prompts ("a photo of a {label}") and pick the label whose text embedding is closest to the image embedding:

```python
image_emb = I(image)
label_embs = T([f"a photo of a {c}" for c in classes])
pred = classes[argmax(image_emb @ label_embs.T)]
```

## Results, briefly

- Zero-shot CLIP matched a fully supervised ResNet-50 on ImageNet, without ever seeing the ImageNet labels.
- Strong transfer across OCR, action recognition, geo-localization, and more.
- Became the de-facto image encoder for generative and multimodal systems.

## The bit that stuck with me

CLIP is the "text meets vision" layer the LLM world got built on. When a model "sees," it usually does so through a CLIP-family encoder. Read it with the Transformer and RAG posts: it pushes the representation-learning playbook from text onto the joint image-text manifold, and it is the reason "describe this image" just works. The caveat I keep returning to: those 400 million pairs were scraped, so whatever the web believed in 2021, the encoder swallowed it too.
